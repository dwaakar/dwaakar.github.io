This study evaluates the status of Water, Sanitation, and Hygiene (WASH) conditions in the Balkhu slum settlement of Kathmandu, which comprises roughly 373 households and an estimated 1,800 residents. An exploratory mixed‑methods design was applied, combining household surveys of 91 families, field observations, and laboratory analysis of five drinking water samples. The assessment focused on determining the adequacy, safety, and reliability of WASH services in a densely populated and environmentally vulnerable informal community. Water in the settlement is supplied through approximately 30 communal storage tanks, each with a capacity of 3,000 to 5,000 liters and filled by private tanker services three to five times per month. Water access is influenced by household financial capacity, resulting in differences in availability. Although most residents use household treatment methods such as cloth straining (88.39 percent), disinfectants (81.38 percent), ceramic filtration (78.03 percent), and boiling (55.83 percent), laboratory results indicated that iron concentrations in all samples averaged around 7 milligrams per liter, which exceeds the national drinking water standard limit of 3 milligrams per liter. Other physicochemical parameters were within acceptable limits. Sanitation conditions show partial improvement. A total of 90.1 percent of households reported access to private water seal latrines, while 9.9 percent continue practicing open defecation. Waste management is the most critical challenge, characterized by irregular municipal collection and widespread disposal of solid waste into the adjacent Bagmati River. Hygiene awareness is relatively strong, yet disease prevalence remains high, with 45.1 percent of respondents reporting diarrhea and 46.7 percent reporting dysentery within the previous three months. The findings indicate that although household level practices are improving, environmental contamination, inadequate waste management, and poor drainage systems continue to pose significant public health risks requiring integrated WASH interventions.
