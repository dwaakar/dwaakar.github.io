Climate change is reshaping hydroclimatic regimes across the Himalayan region, with cascading consequences for water, energy, food, and ecosystem (WEFE) systems. We apply the SWAT+ hydrological model to the Marsyangdi River Basin (MRB) in central Nepal, driven by bias-corrected CMIP6 projections under SSP2-4.5 and SSP5-8.5. An envelope approach spanning warm-wet, warm-dry, cold-wet, and cold-dry climate states captures the full range of plausible futures across mid-century (2041–2070) and end-century (2071–2100).End-century warming reaches up to +3.0 °C under SSP2-4.5 and up to +5.0 °C under SSP5-8.5, with precipitation rising up to 13% and 27% respectively, driven by monsoon intensification. Annual discharge rises by over 30% under SSP2-4.5 and over 50% under SSP5-8.5. Snowfall declines by around a fifth and over a third respectively, eroding the cryospheric buffer that sustains dry-season flows. Pre-monsoon discharge falls under both scenarios, deepening early-season water scarcity even as annual totals rise. Climate extremes intensify across both pathways. Extremely wet-day contributions increase by up to 85% under SSP2-4.5 and more than triple under SSP5-8.5, while daily rainfall intensity strengthens by up to 45% and 55% respectively. Consecutive dry days lengthen by up to 30% under SSP2-4.5 and up to 87% under SSP5-8.5, signaling a future of simultaneous flood and drought risk. These shifts carry compounding sectoral consequences. Dry-season hydropower deficits reach up to 44% under drier futures, losses that wet-season gains cannot offset. Wheat irrigation demand increases substantially under SSP2-4.5 and nearly doubles under SSP5-8.5 by end-century, while paddy remains relatively stable and maize stays effectively rainfed. The findings underscore that the MRB faces a pronounced seasonal mismatch between water availability and cross-sectoral demand that existing frameworks only partially address, reinforcing the urgent need for coordinated, climate-state-informed planning across the WEFE nexus.
